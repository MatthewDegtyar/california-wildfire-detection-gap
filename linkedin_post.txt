An open-data analysis: how often does the standard public satellite wildfire detection product (NASA's FIRMS / VIIRS, the system most US fire agencies have historically relied on) actually catch California wildfires? Scoped to that single open dataset for the 2020 fire season:

For fires contained under 10 acres (93% of all California fires), the public product reports no detection 97% of the time. For the fires it does catch, alerts arrive a median of about 7 hours after the ground-reported alarm, and only for the biggest, most notable ones. Two causes: the sensor can't reliably pick up small fires' heat signatures, and the satellite only passes overhead once or twice a day.

About the images:

First chart: the detection cliff by fire size. The public product catches 3% of fires under a quarter acre, 6% under 10 acres, and only past about a thousand acres does it start reliably reporting. Most California fires never get that big.

Second: a state-wide map of where the blind spots cluster in the public product. The heavily forested northern interior shows the highest miss probability for a hypothetical 100-acre fire. Big caveat: 2020 was an unusual fire year, so the specific map needs more years and other states before anyone bets a decision on it.

Third: how late the public product is when it does eventually catch a fire. About 7 hours median, but only for the biggest notable fires. For everything else, the answer is "never."

Important scope: this is one public dataset, one fire season, one state. I did not evaluate the commercial thermal-imaging constellations (OroraTech, Constellr, FireSat from Muon Space / Earth Fire Alliance) that are explicitly being built to close gaps like this and are either operational or launching in 2026. The point of the analysis was the end-to-end pipeline on open data, not a claim about the broader satellite landscape.

The work: fetch FIRMS detections, join multiple ground-truth sources (FPA-FOD, NIFC perimeters, CAL FIRE incidents), build a calibrated gradient-boost model with honest uncertainty bands, write findings up with explicit limitations.

Code and 12-page write-up: github.com/MatthewDegtyar/california-wildfire-detection-gap
