Spent a few weeks looking at how often satellites actually catch California wildfires. What I found:

→ Satellites never see 97% of California wildfires that get contained under 10 acres — and those small fires are 93% of all California fires in a year.

→ Even when satellites do catch a fire, they're about 7 hours late on average, and that's only for the biggest, most notable ones. By the time anyone gets an alert, half a day of fire growth has already happened.

→ The gap has two causes: satellites can't see a small fire's heat signature even when looking, AND the satellite only passes over any given spot in California once or twice a day.

→ I built a map of where the blind spots cluster across the state — the heavily forested northern interior. The general finding (satellites miss small fires) is rock solid. The specific map needs more years and more states before anyone bets a real decision on it; 2020 was an unusual fire year for CA.

→ The takeaway: there's a real, measurable detection gap in the size range where fires are still small enough to stop. Whatever closes that gap — cameras, lookouts, faster public reporting, aerial patrol, some combination — is filling an operational hole that satellite-only systems demonstrably can't.

Code + 12-page write-up: github.com/MatthewDegtyar/california-wildfire-detection-gap
